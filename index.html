Polish born artist Gosia Walton graduated from Edinburgh College of Art with an MFA in Contemporary Art Practice in 2015. In the same year she was selected to exhibit at the Royal Scottish Academy by Visual Arts Scotland on the basis that her work ‘Best exemplified the fresh and dynamic approach to contemporary and applied and fine arts which VAS seeks to support.’

In 2012 Walton graduated with a BA (Hons) in Interior Architecture from ECA. Her work during this period was primarily concerned with creating three-dimensional spaces and objects that would represent her ideas for installation art and small architectural interventions. As a result of these explorations she recognised a fascination with the role technology can play in the production of art. Laser cutters, 3D and large format inkjet printers have since played a key role in the development of her practice.

In her work she often explores capabilities and boundaries of machinery and technology in relation to the concept of “hand-made.” Through experimentation she tries to give language to the empty or occupied space where digital representations and human intimacy meet. She likes the idea of challenging the perception of the ‘real’ and the ‘artificial’ and the ways in which these constructs are utilized in the making of art.

More recently, Walton has made a series of large-scale drawings, created by a process of etching and cutting onto, and into, fluorescent acrylic sheets using a laser cutter. Through the act of using and misusing machines she hopes to achieve an impression characterised by a contradictory psychological experience; the inherent human traces in the work–gestural lines often associated with the idiosyncratic uniqueness of the creator–alongside the inhuman marks caused by the machine resulting in a sense of tension.

These drawings are a combination of chaotically composed computer generated imagery; lines, simple geometric shapes, letters, numbers and computer keyboard symbols being synthesised into the final work by exporting them in 3D and then modifying them using Adobe Illustrator CC 2014 to form more complex symbols. Through these explorations, Walton has developed a distinctive visual language; one in which objects often appear distorted due to being overworked, or marks appear to float on the surface of the acrylic due to the materiality and refined nature of the material itself.
